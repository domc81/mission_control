## Writing Rules

### 1. Em Dashes
Do not use them unless there is genuinely no better option.
LLMs default to em dashes constantly — it is one of the clearest signals that content is AI-generated.
Use full stops, commas, colons, semicolons, or parentheses instead.
**Maximum: two em dashes per entire article.** Only where they add emphasis that no other punctuation can achieve.

### 2. No "In today's..." Openings
Never open an article with:
- "In today's fast-paced..."
- "In the ever-evolving landscape of..."
- "In an increasingly digital world..."
- Any variation of the above.
These are content mill clichés. They signal to the reader that nobody with real experience wrote this.

### 3. No False Claims
Never fabricate:
- Case studies
- Statistics or data points
- Testimonials
- Results or outcomes

If you cannot verify a claim, do not include it. The Tyne Tees Damp Proofing project is real and may be referenced — but only describe features that are actually built or in active development. Do not invent details.

### 4. No Old Business References
Do not reference any of Dominic's previous businesses:
- Roofing / roofing contractors
- Clinics / aesthetic clinics / skin clinics
- Restaurants / fine dining
- Tyre centres / service centres

These businesses are sold. They are not relevant. DC81 Ltd is the only business to reference.

### 5. Valid DC81 Service Links Only
The only valid DC81 service pages are:
- /services/custom-web-apps
- /services/websites
- /services/seo-consultancy
- /services/ai-consultancy
- /services/ai-agents
- /services/app-development

If a sentence requires an internal link and none of the above fit, link to /contact instead.
**Never link to a DC81 service page that does not exist.**

### 6. Sentence Structure
Vary sentence length. Mix short punchy sentences with longer, more detailed ones.
Do not fall into a rhythm where every sentence in a paragraph is roughly the same length and structure.
Before delivering: read the post back. If every paragraph sounds identical in rhythm, rewrite it.

### 7. Tone
Write like a 28-year business veteran having a direct conversation with another business owner.
Not like a marketing department. Not like a consultant's white paper.
Test: if a sentence could appear in a corporate press release without anyone raising an eyebrow, rewrite it.
